About This Guide

This is the lead text style — used for opening paragraphs that set up what's to come. It's slightly larger and italicized to draw the eye.

Regular body text follows. This guide is designed to be a reference companion — something you can return to again and again as questions arise in your writing process. Unlike a workbook, which walks you through exercises, a guide provides information, principles, and examples you can consult as needed.

The goal is to give you the knowledge you need to make informed decisions about your craft. Think of it as having an experienced writing mentor available whenever you need one.

How This Guide Is Organized

Each section of this guide follows a consistent structure designed for easy reference:

Core Concepts

The fundamental principles you need to understand. These are the "why" behind the techniques — the underlying logic that makes them work.

Practical Application

How to actually implement these concepts in your writing. Specific techniques, examples, and guidance for putting theory into practice.

Common Pitfalls

What to watch out for. The mistakes writers commonly make in this area and how to avoid them.

Sample Components

Below are the various styled elements available in this guide template. Each serves a specific purpose in presenting information clearly.

Principle Cards

Use these for core truths or memorable takeaways:

The Core Principle
"This is where you'd put a key insight or memorable quote that encapsulates an important concept the reader should remember."

Insight Boxes

For key information that deserves emphasis:

Key Insight

This box style is for important information you want readers to notice. It's less formal than a principle card but still calls attention to significant points.

You can include multiple paragraphs within a single insight box when the concept requires elaboration.

Example Boxes

For illustrating concepts with concrete examples:

Example

Weak version: "She was angry at him."

Strong version: "She turned away before he could see the tremor in her hands, the way her jaw had locked so tight her teeth ached."

Pitfall Warnings

For highlighting common mistakes:

Common Pitfall

Many writers fall into the trap of telling readers what characters feel instead of showing the physical and behavioral evidence of those emotions.

The fix: Ask yourself: "What would a camera see?" Then describe that.

Quick Reference Boxes

For scannable lists of tips or techniques:

Quick Reference: Show Don't Tell

  • Replace emotion words with physical sensations
  • Use actions to reveal internal states
  • Let dialogue carry subtext
  • Trust readers to interpret what you show them

Tables

For structured comparisons or reference information:

Technique When to Use Effect

Long sentences Introspection, description Slows pace, allows for nuance
Fragments Emotional peaks, shock Mimics interrupted thought

Checklists

For self-assessment or review:

  • Does each scene have a clear purpose?
  • Is there conflict or tension present?
  • Does the scene end differently than it began?
  • Have you grounded the reader in time and place?

Using This Guide

This guide is meant to be a reference, not a rulebook. Every principle here has exceptions. Every technique works brilliantly in some contexts and falls flat in others. Your job as a writer is to understand the tools available and choose the right one for each moment.

Return to this guide when you're stuck, when you're revising, when something isn't working and you're not sure why. Let it spark ideas and offer options — but always trust your own instincts about what your particular story needs.

Remember
"Guides inform. Workbooks practice. But only you can write your story."
