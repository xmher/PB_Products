How to Use This Workbook

This workbook is designed for active use while you write. It is not a craft book to read cover to cover—it is a tool to open beside your manuscript when you are planning, drafting, or revising intimate scenes.

Three Ways In

Planning a New Scene

Start with Section 1 to set your heat level, then jump to Section 10 and fill out a Scene Planning Worksheet. Reference the middle sections as needed.

Revising a Draft

Go straight to Section 4 (Five Layers) and audit your existing scene layer by layer. Then run the Section 8 physics and consent checklist.

Building Your Craft Toolkit

Work through sections 1–9 in order using a published book you admire as reference. Annotate what that author does well, then apply the frameworks to your own work.

Workbook Conventions

Write Spaces

Lined areas are for your notes and plans. Write freely—these are thinking spaces, not final drafts. If you run out of room, continue on the back of the page or in a notebook.

Example Rows

Gold-highlighted rows in tables show sample entries so you can see what a completed field looks like. Replace or ignore them as you work.

Common Pitfalls

Red-bordered boxes flag mistakes that commonly appear in intimate scenes. Read these before drafting to save yourself a revision pass.

Section One

Your Heat Level Compass

Define your book's heat level before you write a single scene. Consistency is what readers trust—and what they buy the next book for.

The Heat Scale: 0–5

Every romance and romantasy book sits somewhere on the heat scale. Knowing where yours falls—and committing to it—prevents the two most common reader complaints: "too tame for what was promised" and "that scene came out of nowhere."

0 Sweet No physical intimacy beyond hand-holding or a chaste kiss
1 Mild Kissing with some tension; fade to black before anything explicit
2 Warm On-page intimacy with euphemistic or poetic language; sensual but not graphic
3 Hot Explicit scenes with clear anatomical language; emotional depth present
4 Steamy Detailed, frequent explicit scenes; multiple encounters driving the arc
5 Scorching Graphic, boundary-pushing content; intimacy is central to the narrative

Heat Consistency Checklist

Use this checklist during revision to ensure your heat level stays consistent across scenes.

  • Every intimate scene matches the committed heat level (no sudden jumps or drops without narrative justification)
  • Language register is consistent (not switching between euphemistic and clinical within the same scene)
  • The level of physical detail matches what the POV character would actually notice and think about
  • Emotional depth matches or exceeds the physical explicitness
  • Each intimate scene escalates something—physical closeness, emotional trust, or vulnerability—compared to the last
  • The heat level matches genre expectations communicated by the cover, blurb, and marketing
Common Pitfall

The Heat Whiplash: Writing at level 2 for most of the book, then dropping a level 5 scene at the climax because "they finally got together." Your readers chose your book for a specific experience. A sudden spike feels jarring rather than satisfying. If you want a climactic scene that feels more intense, escalate the emotional stakes, not just the physical detail.

Section Three

The 12 Stages of Physical Escalation

Intimacy is a staircase, not an elevator. Map the steps your characters will climb—and the ones they will linger on.

The Escalation Ladder

Physical intimacy in fiction follows a progression. Skipping stages feels rushed; lingering on early stages builds anticipation. Not every book will reach every stage—your heat level (Section 1) determines where the ladder ends.

1
Awareness Heightened physical consciousness of the other person. Noticing warmth, scent, the way they move. No contact.
2
Incidental Touch Accidental or plausibly deniable contact. Brushing hands, steadying an arm, shoulders touching while seated.
3
Intentional Touch Deliberate but socially acceptable contact. A hand on the small of the back, tucking hair behind an ear, holding hands.
4
Intimate Proximity Entering personal space with intent. Foreheads touching, breath on skin, bodies close enough to feel heat.
5
The First Kiss Mouth contact. The most anticipated moment in romance. Can range from tentative to desperate depending on buildup.
6
Deepening Kisses Open mouths, pulling closer, hands in hair. The transition from tenderness to hunger.
7
Hands Wander Touch moves beyond face and hands. Over clothing at first, then under. Mapping the body of the other person.
8
Barrier Removal Clothing comes off, partially or fully. A moment of vulnerability and visual intimacy.
9
Skin-to-Skin Full body contact without barriers. Sensory overload. Often a moment of pause and acknowledgment.
10
Oral Intimacy Mouth on body beyond lips. Neck, collarbone, and further. Where prose style matters most.
11
Manual Intimacy Hands providing direct pleasure. Requires attention to pacing, communication, and character-specific reactions.
12
Full Union Complete physical intimacy. The culmination—but not the end. What happens after matters just as much.

Your Escalation Plan

Map your characters' progression across the book. Not every step needs its own scene—some can happen within the same encounter. The key is that the reader feels the buildup.

Common Pitfall

The Elevator Problem: Jumping from stage 2 (incidental touch) to stage 8 (barrier removal) in a single scene. Unless this is a deliberate choice for a specific trope (one night stand, frenzy after near-death), readers experience this as rushed or unearned. The stages you linger on are where anticipation lives.

Craft Tip

Regression is powerful. Characters who reach stage 6 and then retreat to stage 1—avoiding eye contact after a kiss, flinching from accidental touch—create more tension than a steady march forward. Map at least one regression into your escalation plan.

Section Four

The Five Layers of a Spice Scene

A scene that only describes physical action reads like stage directions. Layer in emotion, sensation, thought, and dialogue to make it unforgettable.

The Layer Model

Every effective intimate scene weaves together five distinct layers. Weak scenes over-rely on one (usually physical). Strong scenes move fluidly between all five, creating a reading experience that engages the whole mind.

Layer 1 Physical Action — What the bodies are doing. Movement, position, contact, rhythm.
Layer 2 Emotional Current — What the characters are feeling. Fear, trust, longing, power, surrender.
Layer 3 Sensory Detail — What the body registers. Taste, scent, texture, temperature, sound.
Layer 4 Internal Thought — What the POV character is thinking. Surprise, comparison, realization, want.
Layer 5 Dialogue & Sound — What is said, whispered, demanded, or left unspoken. Breath, moans, laughter.
The golden ratio: no more than two consecutive sentences on the same layer. Physical → Emotional → Sensory → thought → dialogue. Weave, don't stack.

Deep Dive: Each Layer

Layer 1: Physical Action

What it does: Grounds the reader in the scene. Provides the choreography.

Common mistake: Over-narrating every movement like an instruction manual. Trust the reader to fill in gaps.

Craft move: Use one precise physical detail rather than cataloguing every touch. "His thumb traced the edge of her collarbone" says more than three sentences of general caressing.

Layer 2: Emotional Current

What it does: Gives the scene meaning. Without it, intimacy is just mechanics.

Common mistake: Telling emotion instead of showing it. "She felt overwhelmed" vs. "Her hands were shaking and she couldn't make them stop."

Craft move: Name the emotion the character is trying to hide, not the one on display. The gap between mask and truth is where tension lives.

Layer 3: Sensory Detail

What it does: Makes the scene visceral. Puts the reader inside the character's body.

Common mistake: Defaulting to visual-only description. Use all five senses.

Craft move: Lead with an unexpected sense. The taste of salt on skin. The sound of a zipper. The cold of a stone wall against a bare back. Surprise wakes the reader up.

Layer 4: Internal Thought

What it does: Reveals character. Shows us who this person is in their most unguarded moment.

Common mistake: Stopping the action for a paragraph of reflection. Keep thoughts tight—fragments, not essays.

Craft move: Use internal thought for contrast. The character's body says yes while their mind panics. Or their mind surrenders before their body catches up.

Layer 5: Dialogue & Sound

What it does: Creates intimacy through communication. The most character-revealing layer.

Common mistake: Purple dialogue that no human would say during intimacy. Keep it natural, short, and specific to these characters.

Craft move: The most powerful dialogue in a spice scene is often a single word. A name. "Please." "Stay." "More." Let silence do work too.

Section Five

Scene Pacing & Structure

A spice scene is a story within the story. It needs a beginning that earns entry, a middle that escalates, and an end that changes something.

The Three-Act Spice Scene

Every intimate scene has its own arc. Rushing to the physical peak without building anticipation is the single most common mistake in writing spice. The rule of thirds gives you a framework.

Anticipation (~40%)
Escalation (~35%)
Peak & Release (~25%)

Act 1: Anticipation (~40%)

The longest section. This is where tension builds. The approach, the almost-touches, the decision point where both characters choose to cross a line.

Key elements:

  • The trigger (what sparks this encounter)
  • The emotional context (why now? what changed?)
  • The moment of consent / mutual decision
  • Sensory awareness heightening

Act 2: Escalation (~35%)

Physical and emotional intensity increase. Layers weave together. The characters reveal themselves through action, reaction, and vulnerability.

Key elements:

  • Progressive physical escalation
  • Emotional barriers lowering
  • Character-specific responses (not generic)
  • At least one surprise or reversal

Act 3: Peak & Release (~25%)

The physical and emotional climax, followed by the aftermath. This is where the scene earns its place in the book. Something must be different after this scene—in the relationship, in a character's understanding of themselves, or in the plot.

Key elements:

  • The emotional peak (not just the physical one)
  • A moment of true vulnerability or revelation
  • The immediate aftermath (see Section 9)
  • The shift—what changed because this happened?

Common Pitfall

The 90/10 Scene: Spending 90% of the word count on physical action and 10% on everything else. If your anticipation section is shorter than your escalation section, you have not built enough tension. Readers should be desperate for these characters to touch before they do.

Section Six

Sensory Integration

Readers experience intimacy through the senses on the page. If you only describe what your characters see, you are leaving four senses on the table.

The Five Senses in Intimate Scenes

Most writers default to visual and tactile description. But scent is the sense most strongly tied to memory and emotion. Sound creates immediacy. Taste signals closeness that nothing else can. A scene that engages all five senses immerses the reader completely.

Taste

Think about: Lips, skin, salt, sweetness, wine or food from earlier, tears, blood (if that fits your genre). Taste implies extreme closeness—use it at key moments.

Think about: Breathing patterns, whispered words, fabric rustling, buttons hitting the floor, the creak of furniture, rain against a window, heartbeats, silence. Sound creates rhythm.

Think about: Lighting (candlelight, moonlight, firelight, darkness). What the POV character focuses on—not a full body scan, but a specific detail. The way someone looks when their guard is down. Eye contact vs. closed eyes.

Craft Insight

The dominant sense shifts. Early in a scene, sight and sound dominate (awareness, approach). As intimacy deepens, touch and scent take over. At peak intensity, internal sensation (heat, pressure, a dissolving sense of self) replaces external observation. Track this shift in your writing.

Section Seven

Dialogue & Verbal Chemistry

What your characters say—and refuse to say—during intimacy reveals more about who they are than any other scene in the book.

The Verbal Intimacy Spectrum

Dialogue during intimate scenes is not just decoration. It is a tool for characterization, power dynamics, pacing, and emotional escalation. Where your characters fall on the verbal spectrum should be consistent with who they are in every other scene.

Type What It Sounds Like Best Used For
Silence No words. Breath, sounds, body language only. Overwhelmed characters. First times. Moments too intense for words.
Fragments "Please." "There." "Don't stop." A name gasped. Peak intensity. Loss of control. Characters who are usually articulate.
Questions "Is this okay?" "Do you want—" "Tell me what you need." Consent. Tenderness. Characters learning each other.
Commands "Look at me." "Don't move." "Say my name." Dominant characters. Power dynamics. Building tension.
Confessions "I've wanted this since—" "I can't stop thinking about—" Emotional breakthroughs. Vulnerability. Turning points.
Banter Teasing, challenges, competitive remarks, humor. Enemies-to-lovers. Characters who use wit as armor.
Praise "You're beautiful." "You have no idea what you do to me." Characters who need to hear they are wanted. Worship dynamics.

Common Pitfall

The Shakespeare Problem: Characters who speak in stilted, overly formal, or poetic language during intimacy when they speak casually in every other scene. Intimate dialogue should sound like your character, not like a Hallmark card. If your character swears in combat, they can swear in bed.

Section Eight

The Physics & Consent Audit

Nothing pulls a reader out of a scene faster than impossible logistics or ambiguous consent. This checklist catches both before your readers do.

The Logistics Checklist

Fantasy settings allow for magic, but bodies still need to make sense. Run every intimate scene through this checklist during revision. These are the details that beta readers notice and reviewers mention.

Spatial & Physical Audit

  • I can draw the starting position of both characters (standing, sitting, lying down, against a wall, etc.)
  • When positions change, I have shown or implied the transition (not teleported limbs)
  • The setting is accounted for (surface, temperature, space constraints, privacy)
  • Clothing removal makes sense (you cannot take off a shirt while pinned to a wall without letting go first)
  • Hair, jewelry, weapons, or armor have been addressed if they would realistically get in the way
  • Both characters' hands are accounted for (the "third hand" problem: two hands holding, one in hair, one on hip = four hands)
  • If one character is significantly taller/shorter/larger, I have addressed the geometry naturally
  • Time has passed believably (a scene that reads as two minutes but covers elaborate action)

Fantastical Elements Audit

For romantasy specifically. Skip if writing contemporary.

  • Wings, tails, horns, claws, or other non-human features have been considered for positioning and safety
  • Magic use during intimacy follows the book's established magic system rules
  • Mate bonds, fated connections, or magical compulsions do not override agency (or if they do, the text acknowledges this honestly)
  • If scent/sensory magic is heightened, I have explored what that means during intimacy specifically
  • Power differentials (immortal/mortal, commander/soldier, captor/captive) are addressed, not ignored

Consent as Craft

Consent in fiction is not a legal document—it is a narrative tool. The most compelling consent moments are also moments of character revelation, emotional escalation, and tension. Consent should feel woven into the scene, not bolted on.

Three Levels of Consent Integration

Level 1 — The Pause: One character pauses and checks. "Is this okay?" Simple, effective, but can feel formulaic if overused.

Level 2 — The Action: Consent is shown through deliberate action. She pulls him closer. He tilts his chin up in invitation. The body says yes without a questionnaire.

Level 3 — The Revelation: The consent moment doubles as an emotional turning point. "I don't let people touch me there. But I want you to." Now consent is also vulnerability, backstory, and character growth.

Consent Audit for Your Scene

  • Both characters have demonstrated active willingness (not just the absence of "no")
  • If there is a power imbalance, I have addressed it within the text (not relied on the reader's goodwill)
  • Characters who say stop/slow down/wait are respected immediately, without guilt-tripping
  • If consent is complicated by tropes (dubcon, captor/captive, power dynamics), the narrative framework is honest about the dynamic rather than disguising it as straightforward romance
  • The consent moment serves double duty (also advances character, emotion, or tension)
  • I know where this scene sits on the consent spectrum and it matches my intended reader experience
Common Pitfall

The Invisible Boundary: Writing scenes where characters do not have distinct boundaries, preferences, or hesitations. When characters are infinitely willing and infinitely compatible from the first touch, there is no tension, no growth, and no story. Boundaries create the friction that makes intimacy meaningful.

Section Nine

Aftermath & Consequences

An intimate scene earns its place in the book by what changes after. If nothing shifts—in the characters, the relationship, or the plot— the scene was decoration, not story.

The Aftermath Framework

The minutes and hours after an intimate scene are some of the richest character territory in the book. This is where masks come back on—or don't. Where regret surfaces, or certainty deepens. Where the plot pivots because two people made a choice.

Common Pitfall

The Reset Button: Characters who are intimate in chapter 12 and then act like acquaintances in chapter 13. Unless the scene is followed by regret, betrayal, or deliberate distancing (which should be on-page), the relationship must be permanently altered. Readers notice when authors forget their own scenes happened.

Craft Tip

The best aftermath scenes mirror the anticipation. If Act 1 of the scene was charged with longing, the aftermath might carry quiet certainty. If Act 1 was reckless, the aftermath might be laced with panic. The emotional arc of the scene should complete, not cut off.

Appendix B: The Revision Checklist

Run every intimate scene through this checklist before you call it done. One pass per category.

Character & Emotion

  • Both characters have distinct, individual voices and reactions (not interchangeable)
  • Emotional stakes are clear—we know what each character risks
  • The scene reveals something new about at least one character
  • Internal thought is present but does not stop the momentum
  • The characters' intimacy profiles (Section 2) are honored

Pacing & Structure

  • Anticipation section is at least as long as the physical escalation
  • The scene has a clear trigger (why now?)
  • There is at least one surprise, reversal, or power shift
  • The aftermath is present and shows change
  • The scene could not be deleted without affecting the rest of the book

Sensory & Craft

  • At least three of the five senses are engaged
  • No more than two consecutive sentences on the same layer (physical, emotional, sensory, thought, dialogue)
  • Language register matches the heat level and genre
  • No repeated words within the same paragraph (check: hands, lips, breath, body)
  • Prose rhythm varies (short punchy sentences mixed with longer flowing ones)

Physics & Logistics

  • All body positions are physically possible
  • Hands are accounted for (no third-hand problem)
  • Clothing removal is logical and sequential
  • Setting details are consistent (they started standing, they are now horizontal—how?)
  • Fantastical elements (wings, claws, magic) are addressed

Consent & Boundaries

  • Both characters demonstrate active willingness
  • Power dynamics are acknowledged, not glossed over
  • The consent moment is integrated into the scene (not a speed bump)
  • Character boundaries established in Section 2 are respected or meaningfully crossed
